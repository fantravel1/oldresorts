A Travel Guide for Places That Stopped Trying

OldResorts.com is the first and only travel platform dedicated to unrenovated resorts, vintage hotels, and time-capsule stays across America. We don't review the newest boutique openings or the freshest lobby redesigns. We find the places that haven't changed — and celebrate them for it.

These are hotels where the carpet has a story, the pool deck hasn't been resurfaced since the Carter administration, and the ice machine down the hall is the most reliable thing in your life right now.

"If the lobby has a functioning fountain and a fern that's older than most of the staff, you've found the right place."

Whether you're a writer looking to disappear, a nostalgic traveler chasing a feeling, or someone who just wants a cheap room with a huge empty pool and no vibe pressure — we built this for you.

Our Rating System

The Old Resort Index

Every property is scored across eight dimensions that actually matter. No star ratings. No TripAdvisor algorithms. Just the metrics that tell you whether a resort has successfully resisted the modern world.

Carpet Vintage

0 – 10

How old is the carpet, and how much of it is original? Bonus points for bold patterns and visible wear paths to the ice machine.

Renovation Resistance

0 – 10

Has the property resisted all attempts at modernization? Deductions for any accent walls, Edison bulbs, or reclaimed wood installed after 2010.

Pool Emptiness

0 – 10

Guest-to-pool-square-footage ratio. Higher scores mean more pool, fewer people. A perfect 10 means you will be alone in an Olympic-sized pool.

Lobby Time Warp

0 – 10

When you walk into the lobby, what decade hits you first? Scoring based on original fixtures, furniture, signage, and the general feeling of temporal displacement.

Anonymity Factor

0 – 10

Can you check in and disappear without anyone caring? High scores indicate benign indifference from staff and zero social pressure from other guests.

Ice Machine Reliability

0 – 10

The hallway ice machine is the heartbeat of an old resort. This metric tracks whether it works, how far the walk is, and if the bucket is provided or BYOB.

Vibe Pressure

10 – 0

Inverse scoring — lower is better. Measures how much the resort tries to create an "experience." Perfect score: no programming, no events, no expectations.

Survival Odds

0 – 10

Our estimate of how long this place will remain unrenovated. Factors include ownership stability, local development pressure, and proximity to a Marriott.

A Belief System

The Do Not
Renovate Manifesto

I. If the carpet has survived three decades, it has earned the right to stay.

II. A lobby fountain that still trickles is worth more than a lobby redesign that costs $2 million.

III. Wood paneling is not a problem to be solved. It is a legacy to be honored.

IV. The ice machine is sacred infrastructure. Maintain it. Never relocate it.

V. If no one has complained about the bedspread pattern since 1987, it's a classic.

Everything You Wanted to Know About Old Resorts

The questions people are actually asking about unrenovated hotels, vintage resorts, and time-capsule travel — answered honestly.

Yes — hundreds of them, and they are magnificent in their refusal to change. Across the American coastline, mountain towns, and desert corridors, unrenovated resorts from the 1960s through 1990s continue to operate with original fixtures, carpeting, pool decks, and lobby furniture largely untouched. These aren't abandoned properties — they're active, functioning hotels that simply never saw the need (or had the budget) to modernize. OldResorts.com exists to catalog, review, and celebrate every single one of them before they disappear.
The best vintage resorts still operating are scattered across America's original vacation corridors — the Jersey Shore, Florida's Gulf Coast, the Poconos, the Ozarks, Myrtle Beach, and the Desert Southwest. These are hotels that peaked decades ago and kept the doors open without a design team ever walking through them. We rank and review these properties using the Old Resort Index, measuring factors like carpet originality, renovation resistance, pool emptiness, and lobby time-warp intensity.
Hotels that genuinely feel stuck in the 1970s or 1980s cluster in regions where tourism boomed and then quietly plateaued — Florida's panhandle, Virginia Beach, the Catskills, parts of the Ozarks, and desert motor lodges along old highway routes. These aren't themed "retro" hotels trying to look old; they are old. Expect brown and orange color palettes, textured wallpaper, CRT-era TV mounting brackets, and balconies with original sliding doors that require a specific jiggle to open.
Absolutely. Oversized pools were the crown jewels of mid-century American resorts, and many of these pools remain — often with surprisingly few guests in them. Off-season rates at unrenovated resorts can drop below $60/night, giving you access to Olympic-length pools, multi-level sun decks, and poolside bars that haven't changed their menu since the Reagan administration. The emptiness is the feature. No reserving lounge chairs at 6am, no DJ sets, no influencer photo shoots.
The best time-capsule hotels are the ones that never tried to become something else. These include mid-century motor lodges in the desert Southwest, beachfront high-rises on the Gulf Coast with original terrazzo lobbies, mountain resorts in the Poconos with heart-shaped pools and mirrored ceilings, and sprawling conference-center hotels in the Catskills that once hosted the biggest names in comedy. We score these properties on the Old Resort Index — measuring everything from carpet vintage to ice machine reliability.
If you want the opposite of a curated boutique hotel — no accent walls, no craft cocktail menu, no lobby that doubles as a co-working space — then unrenovated resorts are your answer. These are places with fluorescent hallway lighting, vending machines instead of minibars, and lobbies where the main decor is a large fern and a rack of local attraction brochures. They are gloriously un-Instagrammable in the traditional sense, which is precisely what makes them magnetic to a growing community of anti-trend travelers.
Old resorts are the last great writer's retreat — not the curated kind with residency applications and communal dinners, but the real kind where nobody knows you're there and nobody cares. Desert motor inns, off-season beachfront towers, and mountain lodges in the Catskills or Ozarks offer the perfect conditions: cheap rooms, heavy curtains, no social programming, and the ambient hum of an HVAC system that's been running since 1978. We tag resorts that score high on our Anonymity Factor specifically for this audience.
The last surviving mid-century resorts represent a vanishing category of American architecture and hospitality. These are properties built between roughly 1950 and 1975, during the great boom of automobile tourism and family vacation culture. Many have been demolished or renovated beyond recognition, but a dedicated few remain — often in places where development pressure is low and ownership has stayed in the same family. Our "Do Not Renovate" watchlist specifically tracks these endangered properties so travelers can visit while they still exist in their original form.
The best anti-Instagram hotels are the ones that would never appear on a mood board. They have fluorescent lighting in the hallways, patterned bedspreads that predate social media by decades, and pools surrounded by concrete rather than infinity edges. There's no "content" to create here — just honest, unperformative space. These anti-boutique properties score highest on our Vibe Pressure metric (inverse scale — lower is better), meaning they exert zero social expectations on their guests. You can wear whatever you want, eat continental breakfast in silence, and exist without being perceived.
Yes, and they are magnificent. Piano bars and wood paneling represent the apex of American resort interior design — roughly 1965 to 1985. Many resorts in the Catskills, Poconos, Ozarks, and Florida Gulf Coast still feature original wood-paneled lounges with baby grand pianos, often played on weekend evenings by local musicians who've held the gig for decades. The paneling is usually dark walnut or knotty pine, the lighting is amber, and the drink menu features Amaretto Sours without irony. We specifically track and tag resorts with surviving piano bars.
